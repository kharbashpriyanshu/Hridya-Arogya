What is Cholesterol?
(Good vs Bad Fats in Your Blood)

Cholesterol is a fatty, wax-like substance found in your blood. Your body actually needs some cholesterol to build cells and make hormones. But too much of the wrong kind can cause trouble.

Edited by-Shruti Rai

What is Cholestrol?

Cholesterol is a fat-like substance found in your blood. It travels through your body attached to proteins, forming what’s called lipoproteins.

There are two main types:

    LDL (Low-Density Lipoprotein): “Bad Cholesterol”
  • Builds up on artery walls, forming blockages (plaques).
  • Narrowed arteries make it harder for blood to flow → risk of heart attack or stroke.
    • HDL (High-Density Lipoprotein): “Good Cholesterol”
  • Cleans your blood by carrying LDL back to the liver, where it’s broken down.
  • Protects your heart.

Most people think cholesterol is always “bad.” But that’s not true—your body actually needs cholesterol to build cells and make hormones. The problem starts when there’s too much of the wrong kind.

Why Too Much Bad Cholesterol is Dangerous

High LDL can:

  • Clog arteries (like grease in kitchen pipes).
  • Increase risk of heart attack, stroke, and poor circulation.
  • Force your heart to work harder.

How to Keep Cholesterol Balanced

  • Eat healthy fats → olive oil, nuts, fish, avocado.
  • Avoid trans fats & excess fried food → they raise LDL.
  • Stay active → exercise helps raise HDL (the good kind).
  • Quit smoking & limit alcohol.
  • Get your levels checked—cholesterol problems often have no symptoms.

Final Thought

Think of cholesterol as fat traffic in your blood:

  • Too many “bad” cars (LDL) jam the road.
  • Enough “good” cars (HDL) clear the way.

Keeping the balance means smoother flow, a healthier heart, and a longer life.
